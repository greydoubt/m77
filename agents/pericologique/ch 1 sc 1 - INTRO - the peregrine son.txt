Although the darkness concealed most of his face, Căde looked rather peregrine decked out in the fashionable clothing he preferred. It wasn't just that it was Western attire. It evoked images of what Catholic, Italian men dress themselves in. Inside of Dragoș loomed the threat of this visit being about some sick revelation of his son's sexual hungers. Over the years it had become very clear to Dragoș that the cost of sending all his children abroad to the best boarding schools possible was merely dwarfed by the further expense of keeping them replete with the finer, softer things that Western Europe used to tame its men. 
	Dragoș debated with himself whether the front pikes, the ones used to mount Ottoman heads centuries ago, would be better to adorn with Căde, or if the sharper, thicker ones which formed a vicious halfmaw alongside the back of the sprawling demesne would better serve the purpose. The worry that Căde had grown soft in his years being hidden away from Romanian society and the inner circles of its criminal syndicate kept Dragoș awake at night. One day he would have to deal with the fear every father faces: the day his sons are grown, but not men. 
	It dragged upon his thoughts because of some precedence. He had taken much more care in raising Liviu and Ionut up to his exacting standards of masculinity. Many summers were spent guiding them through the exact motions of how to slaughter first animals and then political enemies. And yet, Dragoș suddenly wondered what was the bigger shame: raising two sons perfectly in his own image only to see them reject the sort of life he had spent all of his days creating, choosing to leave Romania for the promises of the degenerate West, or having to accept as a son who had become artistic and unmanlike. 	The gate stood five meters tall. Even on this moonless night, its bars cast their own shadows. Someone, another voivode, nameless and lost to history, built this mansion and its grounds exactly to the specifications Dragoș always wanted. The two sets of walls -- iron outside and masonry on the interior -- resembled the prison that Dragoș graduated from while he and the rest of Romania struggled beneath Nicolae Ceaușescu's tenure. But instead of confining Dragoș, it served to lock out the village he looked over and collected tribute from.

	It took a brisk walk through his favorite of the five rose gardens that surrounded his mansion to reach the gate. He had the grounds torn up and redone when he took over. The five gardens each bore flowers a different shade of blood: from magenta to burgundy. There were little cobblestone paths that went different, drunken directions but none could take a visitor on a direct path from the mansion to the gates. The confusion that it could potentially instill in an intruder theoretically aided the attack hounds he kept in kennels. 

	Not this night, nor any other night, would anyone actually dare to sneak into Sluger Mansion with anything resembling a chance of making it anything other than a one-way visit. About halfway down the way to the gate, Dragoș wondered if, perhaps, his bastard son had come to pay a visit to murder him. But nothing in their arms-length relationship portended that the anemic little man posed a threat to the master.
